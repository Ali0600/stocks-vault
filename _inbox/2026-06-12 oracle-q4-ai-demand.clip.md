The sharp acceleration in Remaining Performance Obligations (RPO), along with significant upfront customer payments, highlights the strength of that demand.

The database and cloud company beat expectations across the board on a normalized basis and guided for a much larger capex cycle than many had expected, indicating more pressure on free cash flow and greater reliance on debt and equity funding. All of this is meant to support the massive and growing demand for AI cloud capacity. ## What Spooked Investors in Oracle’s Q4

The initial knee-jerk reaction to Oracle’s earnings, with shares falling by double digits in after-hours trading, wasn’t necessarily driven by concerns about cloud demand.

The issue was that Oracle was basically telling the market that demand for AI cloud capacity is massive. However, capturing that demand will require much more capital, more external funding, and continued pressure on free cash flow and margins in the short term. There’s no doubt that this is uncomfortable.

Oracle [guided about $70 billion in net cash capex outlay in FY27](https://www.tipranks.com/news/oracle-plans-to-raise-40b-should-investors-worry), plus another $20 billion–$25 billion in customer prepayments and timing impacts, implying reported capex of roughly $92.5 billion. In fact, that level of capex would exceed the $88.9 billion in consensus revenue expected for Oracle in FY27. This clearly surprised the sell-side, as some analysts had been modeling capex closer to the $67 billion range.

## Oracle’s Funding Needs Are Still the Big Overhang

The problem, as I mentioned in a previous article, is that Oracle, a hyperscaler, still needs to raise equity and debt to fund these massive investments and meet cloud demand. That makes it very different from peers such as Microsoft [MSFT +0.10% ▲](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/msft) , Amazon [AMZN -1.23% ▼](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/amzn) , and Alphabet [GOOGL +0.53% ▲](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/googl) , which have much more balance sheet flexibility.

Oracle expects to [raise around $40 billion in debt and equity](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/oracle-expects-to-raise-40b-in-fy27-from-debt-equity-financing-thefly-news), including its $20 billion at-the-market equity issuance program. This naturally brings back concerns around funding risk, leverage, and potential dilution. Management has already said that FY26 included $43 billion in debt financing and $5 billion in equity financing.

The rest of the cash needed to fund investments in Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) will have to come from operating cash flow. The company generated $32 billion in operating cash flow in FY26, but still reported negative [free cash flow](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/orcl/financials/cash-flow) of $23.7 billion. The trend is that free cash flow will likely remain negative and heavily pressured for now, and there’s no doubt that this weighs on the risk-reward of the thesis.

## The Demand Signal Is Hard to Ignore

Now, looking at the bright side, from an operational standpoint, Oracle delivered an extremely strong quarter. [Revenue](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/orcl/financials/income-statement) came in at $19.2 billion, up 21% year-over-year. OCI revenue came in at $5.8 billion, up 93% year-over-year. The clearest evidence of demand is RPO, or [contracted revenue backlog, which reached $638 billion](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/oracle-says-most-of-rpo-increases-for-q3-q4-were-large-scale-ai-contracts-thefly-news), a significant increase from $553 billion in the previous quarter.

![](https://blog.tipranks.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/Image-11-6-26-at-2-2.png)

Oracle also signed $67 billion in AI infrastructure contracts during the quarter. Meanwhile, the combination of Bring Your Own Hardware (BYOH) and prepaid customer contracts reached $75 billion.

Under BYOH, customers purchase the graphics processing units (GPUs) themselves, while Oracle provides the infrastructure and cloud services. Importantly, management said these contracts come with no margin erosion compared to other contracts. This helps reduce the amount of capital Oracle needs to put up on its own, but it still does not eliminate the funding problem.

## Oracle’s Valuation Now Depends on Execution

That said, prepayments remain a very strong demand signal. After all, no one would pay billions of dollars upfront unless they intended to use the capacity. The less comforting part is that Oracle’s thesis increasingly depends on converting RPO into revenue and maintaining access to debt and equity on reasonable terms.

This perhaps explains how the bear case has shifted. It is no longer mainly about whether there is enough demand. It is now about whether Oracle can build capacity fast enough and monetize its backlog without stretching its balance sheet too far.

This perception of risk naturally tends to spill over into valuation multiples. Currently, Oracle [trades at 25.2x forward earnings](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/orcl/statistics), slightly below the industry average by 3.4% and about 13% above its five-year historical average.

The consensus, following Q4, has become slightly less optimistic about long-term [earnings per share (EPS) growth](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/orcl/earnings), reducing the 2029 EPS forecast by 1% from previous estimates to $15.81. With Oracle trading at around $179 per share, this would imply a 2029 earnings multiple of 11.3x. I view that as attractive for a company that could still be in the middle of a multi-year AI infrastructure revenue ramp.

## Is ORCL a Buy, According to Wall Street Analysts?

The consensus rating for ORCL shares is Strong Buy, based on 32 analyst ratings over the past three months. Of these, 27 are Buy ratings, while five are Hold ratings. The [average post-Q4 price target is $264.72](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/orcl/forecast), implying significant upside potential of 44.58% from the latest share price.

![](https://blog.tipranks.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/ORCL-Stock-Forecast-Price-Targets.jpg)

## Demand Is Real, but Execution Is the Test

While Oracle’s Q4 clearly made the risk-reward less linear, I remain constructive on the stock because the most important part of the thesis continues to improve: demand.

In my view, the market has every reason to worry about capex, cash flow pressure, debt issuance, and even potential dilution. These are real risks that could hurt Oracle’s thesis, and they make Oracle a higher-risk AI infrastructure story than larger hyperscalers with stronger balance sheets. However, I would see those risks as far more concerning if Oracle were spending aggressively ahead of uncertain demand. That does not appear to be the case.

For that reason, I still view ORCL as a **Buy** after Q4. If Oracle can convert even a meaningful portion of its $638 billion RPO into durable cloud revenue, today’s valuation still looks attractive relative to the company’s long-term earnings potential.
