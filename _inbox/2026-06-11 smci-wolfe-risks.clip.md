At least that is the opinion of Wolfe Research analyst George Notter, an analyst who ranks among the top 3% on Wall Street and has initiated coverage of the AI server maker with a Peer Perform (i.e., Neutral) rating without offering a fixed price target. (To watch Notter’s track record, [click here](https://www.tipranks.com/experts/analysts/george-notter))

While Notter concedes that AI “rising tides” help the company, he also says there are “lots of risks here.”

On the plus side, Notter thinks the “asymmetry in the AI infrastructure market is abundantly clear.” Those with supply will benefit in a market where everyone needs AI infrastructure.

But while he remains strongly supportive of the broader market opportunity, he also sees “significant risks” for Super Micro. Expansion among major customers, namely CoreWeave and xAI, is expected to increase customer concentration risk, while the inherently unpredictable nature of AI data center deployments reduces the degree of business visibility.

Moreover, profitability could face headwinds, with customer concentration expected to remain a factor affecting the gross margin mix. Historically, the company has shown a readiness to capture market share even when doing so weighed on profitability. Yet Notter thinks the two margin drivers highlighted by Super Micro, DCBBS (Data Center Building Block Solutions) and Enterprise mix, are not likely to contribute as much to the financial model as investors might be hoping for.

That said, HPE’s withdrawal from lower-margin Neocloud contracts, together with Dell’s continued ability to maintain 5-6% operating margins in AI, could establish a floor for the market. Super Micro also appears to be placing greater emphasis on margin optimization, an area that had not previously received the same level of attention.

But elsewhere, Notter also points to governance issues, regulatory exposure, and an uneven track record in setting and subsequently meeting expectations as additional areas of concern. Not to mention, the DOJ recently indicted co-founder Wally Liaw over allegations related to illegal AI server shipments into China, a situation that could potentially destabilize the company. “We think this is a low probability, albeit worth considering for investors,” the 5-star analyst went on to say.

Potential risks here could include the departure of the company’s auditor, BDO USA, and/or additional turnover within the senior leadership team. Notably, no other executives have been implicated in the indictment at this stage, and Super Micro has stated that its relationship with Nvidia has not been affected. Demand likewise does not appear to have suffered, with backlog levels reported to be at “record highs.”

Nevertheless, Notter says that these factors “limit the multiple we’re willing to place on the business despite the improved growth.”

So, that’s the Wolfe view, but what does the rest of the Street think lies in store for SMCI? Based on a total of 10 Holds, 3 Buys and 2 Sells, the analyst consensus rates the stock a Hold. That said, the $35.55 average price target offers 12-month upside of 22%. ([See SMCI stock forecast](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/smci/forecast))

## The Business Is Executing at Historic Scale

Supermicro builds and delivers AI servers, rack-scale systems, and what it now calls [Data Center Building Block Solutions, or DCBBS](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/super-micro-introduces-dcbbs-blueprints-based-on-nvidia-platforms-thefly-news). The latter are pre-configured, fully integrated data center infrastructure units that customers can deploy at scale while avoiding the friction of assembling components from multiple vendors. Its business model is vertically integrated, and that speed advantage is what has helped generate record revenue over the last two quarters.

Also, the company took an early lead in the Blackwell transition cycle, and that is the reason its order book includes more than [$13 billion in Blackwell Ultra commitments](https://www.tipranks.com/news/company-announcements/supermicro-reports-q1-fy2026-earnings-and-future-outlook).

Meanwhile, its Q3 FY2026 revenue of [$10.2 billion was up 123% year-over-year](https://www.tipranks.com/news/company-announcements/super-micros-earnings-call-ai-boom-cash-crunch). At the same time, we have its gross margin recovery to 9.9% from Q2’s 6.3%, and its non-GAAP earnings per share (EPS) of $0.84 beat the $0.62 consensus estimate by 36%.

I believe that the margin recovery from Q2’s trough is the most important single data point in its recent financial history, because 6.3% was the number bears could point to as evidence of structural deterioration. At 9.9% in Q3, it’s obvious that even though the margins are compressed relative to the 15%–18% range of FY2020–FY2023, they’re recovering. [Full-year FY2026 guidance is close to $40 billion](https://www.tipranks.com/news/super-micro-computer-stock-smci-skyrockets-18-whats-fueling-the-rally), so if Q4 delivers at least $11.5 billion, that will make FY2026 the largest revenue year in Supermicro’s history.

## What the Governance Overhang Means

The governance situation is the reason SMCI’s stock trades where it does today, so I must address it. In March 2026, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) unsealed an [indictment charging three individuals](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/trump-trade-tech-executives-accused-of-smuggling-nvidia-chips-to-china-thefly-news), including SMCI co-founder [Yih-Shyan Wally Liaw](https://www.tipranks.com/experts/insiders/yih-shyan-wally-liaw), with conspiring to [illegally divert approximately $2.5 billion worth](https://www.tipranks.com/news/class-action/super-micro-computer-faces-federal-securities-lawsuit-over-alleged-export-control-violations-and-china-server-sales) of AI servers to China in violation of U.S. export control laws. The servers were powered by Nvidia [NVDA +0.16% ▲](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/nvda) .

After that, the stock fell nearly 30% in a single session and touched a 52-week low of $19.48. It is important to note that [the company itself was not named as a defendant](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/super-micro-issues-statement-on-export-control-indictment-thefly-news).

On its own, I wouldn’t be too bothered, but SMCI has a history with legal sanctions. For example, it previously pleaded guilty to export violations in 2006, paid a $17.5 million [Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) penalty in 2020](https://www.tipranks.com/news/article/super-micro-assessing-the-potential-risk-and-reward), and saw its auditor, [Ernst & Young, resign in 2024](https://www.tipranks.com/news/the-fly/supermicro-plunges-after-auditor-exits-on-unwillingness-to-back-statements). There’s a bit of a pattern here, and some of the company’s proxy advisors have called for significant leadership changes in response. We cannot dismiss any of these things.

However, what I am arguing is that the market is pricing a scenario, direct corporate indictment or Nvidia supply restriction, that has not yet materialized, and that reflects in the price. Conversely, the company’s compliance response, including a new chief compliance officer, a forensic accounting firm, and board-level changes, is the structural playbook for containing, rather than escalating, legal exposure.

Additionally, we’ve heard [Jensen Huang](https://www.tipranks.com/experts/insiders/jen-hsun-huang) call on Supermicro to [tighten its compliance following the indictment](https://www.tipranks.com/news/nvidia-nvda-ceo-calls-for-tighter-compliance-after-super-micro-executives-forge-documents). In my view, that communication is a warning, not a near-term termination notice, and Supermicro responded by issuing a formal statement on collaboration with Taiwanese authorities to prevent illicit technology diversion. To be clear, an active and public compliance posture does not eliminate the risk, but it meaningfully reduces the probability of the worst-case scenario.

## The Valuation Makes the Risk-Reward Asymmetric

At $31.97, Supermicro is the cheapest AI infrastructure name in the market by almost any metric. SMCI [trades at 0.80x trailing 12-month (TTM) EV/Revenue](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/smci/statistics) on a business on track to grow over 100% year-over-year, compared with Dell [DELL +1.05% ▲](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/dell) at 2x TTM EV/Revenue on 47% growth. On a forward earnings basis, the forward P/E is 14.6x with a PEG ratio of 0.42, so the growth rate justifies the current multiple under any scenario that does not involve direct corporate indictment or supply disruption.

Dell, which is growing more slowly with higher gross margins, trades at around 21x forward earnings. For me, the governance discount, which is the gap between Dell-comparable multiples and SMCI’s current multiple, is close to $30 per share or roughly 40% of the governance-risk-adjusted fair value.

My 12-month price target is $72, and I’m confident that it reflects a modest governance-adjusted re-rating to near 22x forward [non-GAAP EPS](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/smci/earnings). That is close to Dell’s multiple and below the hardware sector median, but it is above the current multiple by enough to reflect a trajectory in which the DOJ matter is contained, and Q4 guidance converts cleanly. That’s about 125% upside from current levels.

Conversely, we can also see the bear case involving direct corporate indictment or Nvidia supply restrictions, which may bring a return to the $19–$25 range.

## Wall Street’s Take

15 analysts cover SMCI with a consensus Hold rating and an [average 12-month price target of $35.55](https://www.tipranks.com/stocks/smci/forecast). That’s a mix of three Buys, 10 Holds, and two Sells. The average analyst price target represents 11.18% upside from the current price.

I understand the Hold rating from most covering analysts because it is hard to assign clean multiples to an unresolved governance situation. However, I believe that this is precisely the setup that creates the opportunity for investors willing to underwrite the risk at a price that compensates for it.

## Your Takeaway

SMCI is growing its topline at record levels while still managing to keep a solid bottom line. At the same time, it is trading at a significant discount to its true value because of some fairly serious concerns.

Having said that, my position is that the market is overpricing the worst-case scenario and underpricing a business that is executing at historic scale. SMCI is a **Buy**, but only for investors who understand that the governance outcome is binary and that position sizing should reflect the width of that distribution.
