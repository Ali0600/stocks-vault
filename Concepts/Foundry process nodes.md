# Foundry process nodes

A "process node" (3nm, 2nm…) is roughly how small and dense a fab can print transistors — smaller means more transistors, more speed, and less power per chip. "Leading-edge" is the newest node or two, and only a handful of fabs on Earth can run them, which is what makes the top of the chip stack a chokepoint: the fastest AI chips can only be built where the leading node lives.

The 2026 frontier is 2nm-class, which also marks the switch to gate-all-around (GAA) transistors that wrap the channel on all sides to cut leakage. TSMC's N2 entered volume production in late 2025 — its first GAA node, ~15% denser and ~25–30% lower power than 3nm — with the best yields of the three. Intel's 18A (RibbonFET + PowerVia) is ramping as the US alternative; Samsung's 2nm trails on yield. This is the layer [[TSM]] dominates and [[INTC]] is trying to contest.
